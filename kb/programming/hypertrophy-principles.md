# Scientific Hypertrophy Principles

To maximize skeletal muscle hypertrophy, Project Phoenix recommends structuring routines based on well-established exercise science principles.

## Core Pillars of Hypertrophy

### 1. Mechanical Tension (The Primary Driver)
- **Concept**: Muscles respond to the magnitude and duration of mechanical force. 
- **Application**: Lifting heavy weights or using high-tension digital loading (e.g., `TUT_BEAST` mode) forces target muscle fibers to generate maximal active force, stimulating intracellular signaling cascades (mTOR pathway) for protein synthesis.

### 2. Proximity to Failure (RPE / RIR)
- **Concept**: Muscle fibers must experience high levels of effort to trigger growth.
- **Application**: Sets should be performed with a Rating of Perceived Exertion (RPE) of **7 to 10** (or 0 to 3 Reps in Reserve - RIR). Standard working sets should rarely be performed below RPE 7, as recruitment of high-threshold motor units is sub-optimal.

### 3. Volume and Progressive Overload
- **Concept**: Volume (number of hard sets per muscle group per week) is highly correlated with hypertrophy, up to a point of diminishing returns.
- **Guidelines**:
  - **Weekly Volume**: 10-20 working sets per muscle group per week for intermediate lifters.
  - **Progressive Overload**: Systematically increase weight (per-cable load), reps, or eccentric duration (`TUT` speed) over successive training weeks.

### 4. Frequency & Rest
- **Concept**: Elevated muscle protein synthesis (MPS) remains elevated for 24-48 hours post-workout.
- **Application**: Training each muscle group **2 times per week** is generally superior to training them once per week (split routines), as it maximizes the frequency of elevated MPS. Allow at least 48 hours of recovery between high-intensity sets for the same muscle group.
